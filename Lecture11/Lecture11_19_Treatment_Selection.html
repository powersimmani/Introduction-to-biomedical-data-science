2. Combination Therapies
Combination therapies leverage multiple drugs targeting different molecular pathways simultaneously. Biomarker-guided approaches ensure optimal drug pairing based on tumor molecular profiles, maximizing efficacy while managing toxicity.
Synergistic Effect
Drug A + Drug B > Individual effects
Multi-Pathway Targeting
Simultaneous blockade of compensatory pathways
Resistance Prevention
Reduces emergence of resistant clones
Dose Optimization
Lower doses reduce toxicity
Strategy Types
  • Targeted therapy combinations
  • Immunotherapy + chemotherapy
  • Dual checkpoint inhibition
  • Targeted + immunotherapy
Biomarker Selection
  • PD-L1 expression levels
  • Tumor mutational burden (TMB)
  • Microsatellite instability (MSI)
  • Specific gene mutations
Clinical Examples
  • Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy
  • Dabrafenib + trametinib (BRAF V600E)
  • Nivolumab + ipilimumab
  • Trastuzumab + pertuzumab (HER2+)
Considerations
  • Overlapping toxicity management
  • Drug-drug interactions
  • Sequential vs simultaneous dosing
  • Cost-benefit analysis
Clinical Impact
  • Combination immunotherapy shows 50-60% response rates vs 30-40% monotherapy in melanoma
  • Dual HER2 blockade improves pathological complete response by 15-20% in breast cancer
  • Targeted therapy combinations can extend progression-free survival by 6-12 months
3. Sequential Treatments
Sequential treatment strategies involve carefully planned therapy sequences based on disease evolution, molecular changes, and treatment response. This approach adapts to tumor heterogeneity and dynamic resistance patterns through continuous monitoring.
Phase 1: First-Line Therapy
Initial treatment based on primary molecular profile and tumor characteristics. Standard-of-care therapy with biomarker-guided selection.
Phase 2: Monitoring & Assessment
Continuous evaluation through imaging, liquid biopsies, and biomarker monitoring. Detection of molecular changes and emerging resistance.
Phase 3: Treatment Adaptation
Switch to second-line therapy targeting newly identified pathways or resistance mechanisms. May include combination approaches.
Phase 4: Further Sequencing
Subsequent lines of therapy based on ongoing molecular profiling. Integration of novel agents and clinical trial options.
Key Principles
  • Dynamic disease monitoring
  • Molecular evolution tracking
  • Optimal treatment timing
  • Reserve effective agents
Monitoring Tools
  • Circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA)
  • Serial tissue biopsies
  • Radiographic assessment
  • Tumor marker trends
Examples
  • EGFR TKI → Osimertinib (T790M)
  • Hormone therapy → CDK4/6i → Chemotherapy
  • Anti-VEGF → Immunotherapy
  • First-gen → Second-gen targeted agents
Advantages
  • Addresses clonal evolution
  • Maximizes treatment durability
  • Preserves quality of life
  • Extends overall survival
Clinical Impact
  • Sequential EGFR TKI therapy extends median survival to 3-4 years in EGFR+ lung cancer
  • Liquid biopsy-guided sequencing allows earlier detection of resistance (3-5 months advantage)
  • Optimal sequencing strategies can improve overall survival by 20-30% compared to random sequencing
