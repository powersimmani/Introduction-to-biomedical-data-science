Detailed Biomarker Classification

1
Diagnostic Biomarkers
Disease Detection and Identification
Diagnostic biomarkers are used to detect or confirm the presence of a disease or condition, or to identify individuals with a subtype of the disease. They help clinicians make accurate diagnoses and distinguish between different disease states.
Clinical Examples:
  • Troponin: Elevated levels indicate myocardial infarction (heart attack)
  • HbA1c: Diagnoses diabetes mellitus (≥6.5% indicates diabetes)
  • PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen): Screening tool for prostate cancer
  • HIV Antibodies: Confirms HIV infection
  • BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations: Identifies hereditary breast and ovarian cancer risk
Key Characteristics:
✓ High sensitivity and specificity
✓ Distinguishes diseased from healthy individuals
✓ May identify disease subtypes

Prognostic Biomarkers
Predicting Disease Outcome
Prognostic biomarkers provide information about the likely course of disease in an individual patient, independent of treatment. They help predict clinical outcomes such as disease recurrence, progression, or survival, informing treatment intensity decisions.
Clinical Examples:
  • Oncotype DX score: Predicts breast cancer recurrence risk (low/intermediate/high)
  • Tumor Grade & Stage: Higher grade/stage correlates with worse prognosis
  • TP53 mutations: Associated with poor prognosis in various cancers
  • NT-proBNP levels: Predicts heart failure outcomes and mortality
  • Ki-67 index: High proliferation marker indicates aggressive tumor behavior
Key Characteristics:
✓ Independent of treatment effects
✓ Stratifies patients by risk level
✓ Guides treatment intensity decisions

3
Predictive Biomarkers
Treatment Response Forecasting
Predictive biomarkers indicate the likelihood of a patient responding to a specific therapeutic intervention. They guide personalized treatment selection by identifying which patients will benefit from particular therapies and who should avoid treatments unlikely to work or likely to cause harm.
Clinical Examples:
  • HER2 amplification: Predicts response to trastuzumab (Herceptin) in breast cancer
  • EGFR mutations: Identifies lung cancer patients for EGFR inhibitor therapy
  • KRAS mutations: Predicts non-response to anti-EGFR therapy in colorectal cancer
  • PD-L1 expression: Predicts response to immune checkpoint inhibitors
  • BCR-ABL fusion: Indicates responsiveness to imatinib in CML
Key Characteristics:
✓ Treatment-specific indication
✓ Enables precision medicine approach
✓ Prevents ineffective treatments

4
Pharmacodynamic Biomarkers
Treatment Response Monitoring
Pharmacodynamic biomarkers measure the biological response to therapeutic intervention, showing whether a drug is having its intended effect on target tissues or pathways. They are essential for dose optimization and confirming target engagement during drug development and clinical use.
Clinical Examples:
  • Blood pressure reduction: Response to antihypertensive drugs
  • HbA1c levels: Monitoring glucose control with diabetes medications
  • Tumor shrinkage (imaging): Response to chemotherapy or targeted therapy
  • Cytokine levels: Biological response to immunotherapy
  • Target protein inhibition: Confirms kinase inhibitor activity
Key Characteristics:
✓ Demonstrates drug mechanism of action
✓ Guides dose optimization
✓ Confirms target engagement

5
Safety Biomarkers
Toxicity and Adverse Effect Detection
Safety biomarkers detect potential adverse effects or toxicity related to therapeutic interventions. They enable early identification of organ damage or dysfunction, allowing for timely intervention to prevent serious complications and guide safe dosing strategies.
Clinical Examples:
  • Liver enzymes (ALT/AST): Detect drug-induced liver injury
  • Creatinine/eGFR: Monitor kidney function and nephrotoxicity
  • Troponin elevation: Detects cardiotoxicity from chemotherapy
  • Complete blood count: Monitors for bone marrow suppression
  • QT interval (ECG): Identifies cardiac arrhythmia risk
Key Characteristics:
✓ Early warning of toxicity
✓ Enables dose modification
✓ Protects patient safety

Integration in Clinical Practice

In modern precision medicine, multiple biomarker types are often used in combination throughout the patient journey—from initial diagnosis to treatment selection, monitoring, and safety surveillance. Understanding these distinct classifications enables clinicians to optimize therapeutic strategies and improve patient outcomes through data-driven, personalized care approaches.
