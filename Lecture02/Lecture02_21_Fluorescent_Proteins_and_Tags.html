The Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP) family originated from the jellyfish Aequorea victoria and revolutionized cell biology. These proteins spontaneously form a chromophore from their own amino acid residues, eliminating the need for external cofactors. The family has been engineered to span the visible spectrum, providing researchers with a palette of colors for cellular imaging.
Key Features:
  • Nobel Prize 2008: Awarded to Osamu Shimomura, Martin Chalfie, and Roger Tsien for the discovery and development of GFP
  • Autocatalytic: Forms fluorescent chromophore without external enzymes or cofactors
  • Excitation/Emission: Original GFP - 395nm (UV) & 475nm (blue) / 509nm (green)
  • Variants: Enhanced GFP (EGFP), superfolder GFP (sfGFP) for improved stability and brightness
  • Beta-barrel structure: 11-stranded Î²-barrel protects the chromophore from the environment

Genetic encoding allows fluorescent proteins to be fused directly to proteins of interest through molecular cloning. The DNA sequence encoding the fluorescent protein is inserted into the gene of the target protein, resulting in a single polypeptide chain that carries both functionalities. This approach enables real-time tracking of protein localization, dynamics, and interactions in living cells.
Advantages & Considerations:
  • No external addition required: Once transfected, cells produce tagged proteins autonomously
  • Minimal perturbation: Small size (27 kDa for GFP) and careful linker design reduce functional disruption
  • Live cell compatible: Non-toxic, stable expression enables long-term imaging
  • Spatial precision: Tags can be placed at N-terminus, C-terminus, or internal positions
  • Validation needed: Must confirm that tagged protein retains normal function and localization
  • Linker design: Flexible peptide linkers (e.g., GGGS repeats) prevent steric hindrance

Minimal Spectral Overlap = Clear Signal Separation
Multicolor imaging leverages the spectral diversity of fluorescent proteins to simultaneously visualize multiple cellular components in the same sample. By carefully selecting fluorescent proteins with distinct excitation and emission spectra, researchers can track multiple proteins, organelles, or cellular events in real-time without cross-contamination of signals. This powerful technique has become essential for understanding complex cellular processes involving multiple interacting components.
Technical Requirements & Benefits:
  • Spectral separation: Proteins must have minimal overlap in emission spectra (typically >30nm apart)
  • Modern variants: Optimized proteins like mTurquoise2, mVenus, and mCherry offer superior brightness and photostability
  • Simultaneous acquisition: Multiple fluorescence channels can be imaged concurrently or sequentially
  • Co-localization studies: Determine if proteins occupy the same cellular location
  • Dynamic interactions: Track how different proteins move and interact over time
  • 3-4 color maximum: Practical limit due to spectral overlap and detector limitations

Photoswitchable fluorescent proteins can be toggled between fluorescent and non-fluorescent states using specific wavelengths of light. This unique property forms the basis of superresolution microscopy techniques like PALM (Photoactivated Localization Microscopy) and STORM (Stochastic Optical Reconstruction Microscopy), which overcome the diffraction limit of conventional light microscopy (~200nm) to achieve resolutions of 20-50nm.
Key Photoswitchable Proteins & Mechanisms:
  • Dronpa: Reversibly photoswitchable between fluorescent (488nm) and dark states (405nm activation)
  • mEos family: Photoconvertible from green (516nm) to red (581nm) with 405nm light - irreversible switch
  • PA-GFP: Photoactivatable - increases fluorescence 100-fold upon 405nm irradiation
  • Dendra2: Green-to-red photoconversion with improved photostability and brightness
  • Single-molecule detection: Sparse activation enables precise localization of individual molecules
  • Resolution breakthrough: Achieves 10-20Ã— better resolution than conventional microscopy
Superresolution Techniques:
PALM (Photoactivated Localization Microscopy)
Uses photoactivatable proteins to sequentially activate, localize, and bleach individual molecules
STORM (Stochastic Optical Reconstruction)
Employs photoswitchable probes to create stochastic blinking for superresolution imaging
Pulse-Chase Experiments
Photoconvert specific protein pools to track their fate separately from newly synthesized proteins
Subcellular ROI Marking
Selectively photoactivate proteins in specific cellular regions to study local dynamics

Photoswitchable proteins have revolutionized our ability to visualize cellular structures at the nanoscale level. The 2014 Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded to developers of superresolution microscopy techniques that rely on these remarkable proteins.
